# A Beginner's Guide to Building Your First PC

Building your own PC is more approachable than most people expect. You don't need specialist tools or advanced technical knowledge — just patience, a clear plan, and the right components. This guide walks through everything you need to know before you buy your first part.

## Why build instead of buying pre-built

The main reason to build your own PC is value. For the same budget, a self-built PC will almost always outperform a pre-built one because you're not paying for assembly labour, a branded case, or components chosen for margin rather than performance.

Building your own PC also gives you full control over every component. You choose the case, the cooling, the storage capacity, and the aesthetics. And when something needs upgrading in two years, you know exactly what's in there and how to replace it.

The downside is time and the risk of making mistakes. Most mistakes are recoverable, but it's worth going in with realistic expectations.

## The core components you need

Every PC build requires the same set of core components.

**CPU (Central Processing Unit)** — the brain of the PC. AMD's Ryzen 9000 series and Intel's Core Ultra 200S series are the current generation. Your choice of CPU determines which motherboard socket you need.

**Motherboard** — connects all your components together. Must be compatible with your CPU socket. AMD Ryzen 9000 series CPUs use AM5 socket motherboards. Intel Core Ultra 200S CPUs use LGA1851 socket motherboards. These sockets are not interchangeable.

**RAM** — temporary memory used while the PC is running. Both current AMD and Intel platforms require DDR5 RAM. 32GB is a comfortable amount for gaming and general use. 64GB is worth considering for content creation or running local AI models.

**GPU (Graphics Processing Unit)** — handles graphics rendering and is the most important component for gaming performance. Also used for machine learning and AI workloads. The GPU is usually the most expensive component in a gaming build.

**Storage (SSD)** — where your operating system, applications, and files are stored. NVMe SSDs are significantly faster than older SATA SSDs and are the standard choice for a primary drive today. PCIe 4.0 NVMe drives offer excellent performance for most users. PCIe 5.0 drives are faster but more expensive and the speed difference is not noticeable in everyday use.

**PSU (Power Supply Unit)** — converts mains power to the voltages your components need. Choose a PSU with enough wattage to cover your components with some headroom. A mid-range build with a GPU drawing around 250W typically needs a 650W PSU. A high-end build with a GPU drawing 360W needs at least an 850W PSU.

**Case** — houses all your components. Choose a case that fits your motherboard form factor (ATX is the most common) and has good airflow. Make sure the case is long enough to fit your GPU — high-end GPUs can be very large.

**CPU Cooler** — keeps the CPU at a safe temperature. Some CPUs include a stock cooler which is adequate for non-overclocked use. High-end CPUs like the Ryzen 9 9950X or Core Ultra 9 285K generate a lot of heat and require a more capable aftermarket cooler or an all-in-one liquid cooler.

## Compatibility is the most common source of mistakes

The most important compatibility check is CPU and motherboard socket. An AM5 CPU will not fit in an LGA1851 motherboard and vice versa. Always verify socket compatibility before purchasing.

RAM type is the second most important check. Both current AMD AM5 and Intel LGA1851 platforms require DDR5 RAM. DDR4 RAM is not compatible with these platforms.

GPU length is worth checking. Measure the interior length of your case and compare it to the GPU's dimensions before buying. High-end GPUs like the RTX 5080 are physically large and will not fit in all cases.

PSU wattage must cover the total power draw of your system with headroom to spare. Add up the TDP of your CPU and GPU and add around 100-150W for the rest of the system. Choose a PSU rated at least that high.

## A suggested mid-range build

For a capable 1440p gaming PC, a reasonable starting point is:

- CPU: AMD Ryzen 7 9700X
- Motherboard: MSI MAG B850 Tomahawk WiFi
- RAM: Corsair Vengeance DDR5-6000 32GB
- GPU: AMD Radeon RX 9070 XT
- Storage: Samsung 990 Pro 2TB NVMe SSD
- PSU: 750W 80+ Gold rated unit

This combination offers strong 1440p gaming performance and all components are confirmed compatible with each other.

## The build process

The actual assembly follows a consistent order: install the CPU into the motherboard, install the RAM, mount the motherboard in the case, install the PSU, install storage, connect power cables, install the GPU last. Most motherboard manuals walk through this process clearly.

The most common beginner mistake is not seating components fully. RAM and GPUs require firm pressure to click into their slots. If the system doesn't boot, reseating RAM is the first thing to check.

Take your time, work in a well-lit space, and don't force anything that isn't going in smoothly.
