# How to Choose a Monitor: Resolution, Refresh Rate, and Panel Type Explained

Choosing a monitor is more confusing than it should be. The spec sheets are full of numbers and acronyms, and manufacturers don't always make it easy to understand what actually matters for your use case. This guide breaks down the key specs and explains what to look for depending on how you plan to use your monitor.

## Resolution

Resolution determines how many pixels are on screen. More pixels means a sharper image, but also requires more GPU power to drive at high frame rates.

**1080p (1920x1080)** is still the most common resolution for budget monitors and laptops. It looks fine on smaller screens but starts to look soft on screens larger than 27 inches. GPU requirements are modest.

**1440p (2560x1440)** is the sweet spot for most desktop users right now. It's noticeably sharper than 1080p, looks great on 27-inch screens, and doesn't require a top-tier GPU to run modern games at high settings. Most mid-range GPUs like the RX 9070 XT handle 1440p gaming comfortably.

**4K (3840x2160)** delivers a very sharp image and is worth it for large screens, professional creative work, or if you have a high-end GPU capable of driving it. At 27 inches the difference over 1440p is visible but not dramatic. At 32 inches and above, 4K becomes more clearly worthwhile. For gaming at 4K you need a powerful GPU — the RTX 5080 is the minimum worth considering for a good 4K gaming experience.

**Ultrawide (3440x1440 or 5120x1440)** monitors offer a wider field of view for gaming and more screen real estate for productivity. The Samsung Odyssey G9 at 5120x1440 is the extreme end of this — it's immersive for gaming but requires a very powerful GPU and a large desk.

## Refresh rate

Refresh rate is how many times per second the monitor updates the image, measured in Hz. A higher refresh rate makes motion look smoother and more responsive.

**60Hz** is adequate for everyday use, productivity, and watching video. It feels noticeably less smooth for fast-paced gaming compared to higher refresh rates once you've experienced the difference.

**144Hz and 165Hz** is where most gaming monitors sit. The jump from 60Hz to 144Hz is very noticeable. For competitive gaming or anyone who values responsive input, 144Hz should be considered a minimum.

**240Hz and above** is for serious competitive gamers who want the lowest possible input lag and the smoothest possible motion. The difference between 144Hz and 240Hz is less dramatic than the jump from 60Hz to 144Hz, but it is real.

For high refresh rate gaming to be meaningful, your GPU needs to be able to produce enough frames to match. A 165Hz monitor only feels different from a 60Hz monitor if your GPU is actually rendering above 60fps.

## Panel type

The three common panel types each have different strengths.

**IPS (In-Plane Switching)** panels offer the best colour accuracy and the widest viewing angles. Colours look consistent from off-axis, which matters for creative work and for monitors used by multiple people. IPS panels have historically had slightly slower response times than TN panels, but modern IPS monitors are fast enough for gaming. The LG 27GP850-B and Dell Ultrasharp U2725D are both IPS panels.

**VA (Vertical Alignment)** panels offer the best contrast ratio of the three types, producing deeper blacks. This makes them well suited to dark environments and HDR content. The trade-off is that VA panels can exhibit motion blur more than IPS in fast-moving scenes. The Samsung Odyssey G9 uses a VA panel, which suits its large curved format.

**TN (Twisted Nematic)** panels have the fastest pixel response times and are found in some competitive gaming monitors. Colour accuracy and viewing angles are weaker than IPS. TN panels have become less common as IPS response times have improved.

## What to look for based on your use case

**For gaming:** prioritise refresh rate and resolution appropriate to your GPU. An IPS panel at 1440p and 165Hz like the LG 27GP850-B is an excellent all-round gaming monitor. Match the monitor's resolution to what your GPU can drive — there's no benefit to a 4K monitor if your GPU can't produce enough frames.

**For creative work and development:** prioritise colour accuracy and resolution. The Dell Ultrasharp U2725D at 4K with 98% DCI-P3 coverage is a strong choice. USB-C with power delivery is a useful feature if you connect a laptop. Refresh rate matters less for this use case.

**For both gaming and work:** an IPS panel at 1440p with a 144Hz or 165Hz refresh rate is the most versatile choice. It's sharp enough for productivity, accurate enough for casual creative work, and fast enough for gaming.

## Other specs worth checking

**HDR support** varies significantly in quality. Entry-level HDR400 certification (found on many gaming monitors) makes little visible difference. Meaningful HDR requires at least HDR600, ideally HDR1000 with local dimming.

**USB-C with power delivery** is worth having if you use a laptop. It lets you connect, charge, and display video through a single cable.

**Adjustability** matters more than people expect. A monitor with height, tilt, and swivel adjustment is easier to set up ergonomically than a fixed-stand model.
